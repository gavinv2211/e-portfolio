Initial Post (Unit 1)
A chosen case study provided by the Association of Computing Machinery.

The chosen case study “Malicious Inputs to Content filters” (ACM, N.D.). Examines the ethical challenges faced by the developers of Blocker plus, an automated internet content filter created to comply with the U.S. Children’s Internet Protection Act (CIPA). Initially the software was designed with a centrally controlled blacklist that was maintained by the creators of Blocker plus. Due to the challenges of manually updating the blacklist, the creators begun to implement machine learning techniques to automate the identification of inappropriate content.

User feedback was leveraged to automate the identification of inappropriate content and enhance model efficiency. However, activist groups exploited this feedback mechanism by submitting malicious inputs, causing the system to incorrectly classify and block legitimate content. The developers failed to implement safeguards against intentional misuse, violating Principle 2.5 of the ACM code of ethics. Which stipulates that in systems where future risks cannot be accurately predicted, frequent reassessment of risks should be conducted, or the system should not be deployed (ACM, 2018). Furthermore, the developers did not disclose the system’s limitations to the public, violating Principle 2.7 which states that the limitations, vulnerabilities and significant issues of computer systems should be communicated to the public clearly and respectfully (ACM, 2018).

The faulty filter unjustly blocked legitimate information on topics such as gay and lesbian marriage, vaccination, and climate change violating the BCS’s code of conduct principle, “You make IT for everyone”. This principle states that activities should be carried out without discrimination of sexual orientation, religion, or age and that equal access to the benefits of IT should be actively promoted (BCS, 2022).

Summary (Unit 3)

The chosen case study “Malicious Inputs to Content filters” (ACM, N.D.), discussed how the developers of Blocker Plus failed to fulfil their ethical obligations as outlined in the ACM code of ethics and BCS code of conduct. The discussion highlights specific ACM principles that were violated and identifies key BCS code of conduct principles that were disregarded.

While the post could have considered how developers could have avoided violating principles by following the principles of section 2 in the ACM code of ethics which outlines specific considerations of professional responsibility, I do believe that ethical responsibility should not fall solely on the technical professionals who developed the software. Instead, the company as a whole must be held accountable.

Section 3 of the ACM Code of Ethics emphasizes the role of leadership in upholding ethical standards. While these principles apply to computing professionals, leadership has a crucial responsibility to uphold, promote and enforce ethical practices across the organization (ACM, 2018).

Furthermore, principle 3.1 states that leaders must ensure the well-being of all individuals affected by computing including customers and colleagues (ACM, 2018). This reinforces that ethical responsibility extends beyond developers to company leadership, who also failed to adhere to ACM principles. Ensuring ethical compliance is not just a technical obligation, but an organizational commitment that must be embedded in the company and in its decision-making.

Reflection
Discussing the challenges faced by developers offers a practical approach to exploring and sharing ideas around ethical behavior in the workplace. It encourages you to put yourself in the developer's position, reflecting crically how you might respond to ethical dilemmas in a professional setting. As professionals, we sometimes focus solely on completing tasks which was assigned to us. However, we all share a responsibility to act ethically and speak up when necessary. Ethical awareness shouldn’t be limited to leadership, every professional should be familiar with and guided by the relevant code of ethics.
